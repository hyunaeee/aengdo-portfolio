## Diagnostic guidance (2025-06) · Screening intervals, risk stratification, and dense breasts

Scope: imaging of asymptomatic people for early detection, and how personal risk and
breast density modify the interval and the modality. Nothing here applies to a patient
with a symptom or to surveillance after a cancer diagnosis.

1. Screening presumes no symptoms. A palpable lump, skin change, or pathologic nipple
   discharge moves the patient into the diagnostic pathway immediately, whatever the
   date of her last normal screen. "She had a clear mammogram in March" is not a reason
   to wait.
2. For average-risk women, mammography is offered annually or biennially from the
   fifth decade, with many programs opening the discussion at 40. The choice of interval
   is a shared decision that should name the harms explicitly: recall for false
   positives, benign biopsy, and overdiagnosis of indolent disease.
3. Continue screening while the woman is in reasonable health and has a life expectancy
   of roughly ten years or more. Stop by comorbidity, not by birthday alone.
4. Digital breast tomosynthesis improves cancer detection and lowers recall compared
   with two-dimensional mammography, with the largest gain in non-fatty breasts.
5. Density is reported as a four-level category, from almost entirely fatty through
   heterogeneously dense to extremely dense. Density does two separate things: it masks
   cancers on mammography, and it is itself a modest independent risk factor.
6. For heterogeneously or extremely dense breasts in a woman who is otherwise average
   risk, supplemental whole-breast ultrasound or contrast-enhanced mammography finds
   additional cancers but raises false positives substantially. Offer it as a shared
   decision, not as an automatic add-on.
7. High risk is defined by a formal model that incorporates family structure and age at
   diagnosis in relatives, not by a vague sense that the history is bad. A lifetime risk
   at or above roughly 20 to 25% qualifies for intensified screening.
8. High-risk protocol is annual contrast-enhanced breast MRI added to annual
   mammography. For germline pathogenic variant carriers, MRI typically begins around
   age 25 to 30, with mammography deferred until about 30.
9. Women irradiated to the chest between ages 10 and 30, classically for Hodgkin
   lymphoma, begin annual MRI and mammography about eight years after radiotherapy and
   not before age 25.
10. Prior atypical ductal hyperplasia or lobular carcinoma in situ confers elevated
    risk. Consider supplemental MRI and discuss risk-reducing endocrine therapy.
