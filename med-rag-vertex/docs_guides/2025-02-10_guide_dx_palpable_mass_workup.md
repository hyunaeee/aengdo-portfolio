## Diagnostic guidance (2025-02) · Workup of a new discrete palpable breast mass

Scope: the first diagnostic encounter for an adult presenting with a new, discrete,
palpable lump. This document does not cover asymptomatic screening intervals, nipple
discharge without a mass, or post-treatment surveillance imaging.

1. Triple assessment means three concurrent legs — clinical examination, targeted
   imaging, and tissue sampling when indicated. A lump is not cleared until all
   applicable legs agree.
2. Imaging is chosen by age. Under approximately 30 years, begin with targeted
   ultrasound of the palpable site; add mammography only if ultrasound is suspicious
   or indeterminate. At 30 years and above, obtain diagnostic mammography (with
   tomosynthesis where available) together with targeted ultrasound.
3. During pregnancy or lactation, ultrasound is the first test at any age. Mammography
   remains permissible when needed; fetal dose from a shielded diagnostic study is
   negligible, and pregnancy is not a reason to defer diagnosis of a suspicious mass.
4. Place a radiopaque skin marker over the palpable abnormality so the radiologist
   images the same lesion the clinician felt. An unmarked study risks reporting a
   different, incidental finding.
5. Normal imaging does not exclude malignancy. If the mass is clinically suspicious —
   hard, fixed, irregular, with skin dimpling or nipple retraction — proceed to tissue
   sampling despite a negative ultrasound and mammogram.
6. Core needle biopsy is preferred over fine-needle aspiration because it preserves
   architecture and permits grading plus ER, PR, and HER2 testing. Reserve aspiration
   for draining a symptomatic simple cyst or sampling a suspicious axillary node.
7. A simple anechoic cyst with posterior enhancement needs no biopsy. Aspirate only for
   symptoms; if the aspirate is bloody or a residual mass persists after drainage,
   biopsy the residuum.
8. Complicated cysts, complex cystic-and-solid lesions, and any solid mass that is not
   a classically benign fibroadenoma warrant tissue diagnosis.
9. Deploy a marker clip at biopsy so the site can be relocated for surgery or after
   neoadjuvant therapy.
10. Every benign biopsy result must be reviewed for radiologic-pathologic concordance.
    Discordant benign histology — for example, fibrocystic tissue reported from a
    spiculated mass — requires rebiopsy or excision, not reassurance.
11. Safety-net explicitly: give the patient a defined re-examination interval and
    instruct her to return sooner if the lump enlarges or the skin changes.
