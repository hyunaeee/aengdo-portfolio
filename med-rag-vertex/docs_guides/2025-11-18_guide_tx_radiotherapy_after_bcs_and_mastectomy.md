## Treatment guidance (2025-11-18) · Radiotherapy after breast-conserving surgery and after mastectomy

Scope: post-operative locoregional radiotherapy in non-metastatic disease. This note covers
indications and volumes. It does not cover systemic therapy selection.

**After breast-conserving surgery, radiotherapy is part of the operation, not an optional
extra.** Lumpectomy without irradiation carries a substantially higher rate of in-breast
recurrence, so whole-breast radiotherapy is the default for essentially every patient who
keeps her breast, including those with DCIS alone. Moderately hypofractionated schedules —
around fifteen to sixteen fractions over three weeks — are the standard of care for most
patients and are not an inferior compromise. A boost to the tumour bed is added for younger
patients, high-grade disease, or close margins, and can reasonably be omitted in an older
patient with low-grade, widely clear disease.

**Two situations are genuinely different from the default.** In a woman over about 65–70 with
a small, node-negative, hormone-receptor-positive, HER2-negative tumour excised with clear
margins, omitting whole-breast radiotherapy in favour of endocrine therapy alone is a
legitimate shared decision; local recurrence risk rises modestly while survival appears
unchanged, and the choice hinges on how the patient weighs those. Separately, partial-breast
irradiation is an option for carefully selected low-risk, node-negative tumours, and is not
appropriate for higher-risk or node-positive disease.

**After mastectomy, radiotherapy is indicated rather than routine.** Post-mastectomy
radiotherapy is recommended for four or more involved axillary nodes, for T3–T4 primaries,
for positive or persistently close margins, and for inflammatory breast cancer. With one to
three involved nodes it is discussed case by case, weighing age, grade, lymphovascular
invasion and receptor subtype. Where the axilla is treated, regional nodal irradiation
including the supraclavicular and internal mammary chains may be added; this is a separate
decision from chest wall treatment.

**Practical points.** Radiotherapy follows chemotherapy rather than running concurrently with
anthracyclines. Endocrine therapy may run alongside irradiation. Immediate reconstruction
does not remove an indication for radiotherapy, but it changes planning and reconstructive
sequencing, so the plastic surgery team should be involved before the mastectomy, not after.
Left-sided treatment should use cardiac-sparing technique such as deep inspiration breath
hold.

Treatment decisions belong to the multidisciplinary team and the patient; decision-support
tools do not prescribe.
