## Treatment guidance (2025-05-06) · Endocrine therapy: the tamoxifen versus aromatase-inhibitor decision

Scope: adjuvant endocrine therapy for hormone-receptor-positive invasive breast cancer. This
note covers only the choice between a selective estrogen receptor modulator and an aromatase
inhibitor, and the information required before that choice can be made.

**Menopausal status is a required input, not a refinement.** The two drug classes work by
different mechanisms, and the mechanism of one of them fails outright in the wrong patient.
Tamoxifen blocks the estrogen receptor and works regardless of where estrogen comes from.
Aromatase inhibitors — anastrozole, letrozole, exemestane — work by blocking peripheral
conversion of androgens to estrogen, which is the dominant source of estrogen only after the
ovaries have stopped producing it. Given alone to a patient with functioning ovaries, an
aromatase inhibitor does not adequately suppress estrogen and can provoke reflex ovarian
stimulation. Prescribing one without established menopausal status is therefore not a
cautious approximation; it is a potentially ineffective treatment.

**Consequently, an endocrine regimen cannot be selected from receptor status alone.** If a
request specifies "ER-positive, start endocrine therapy" without menopausal status, the
correct response is to decline to name an agent and to ask for the missing information. The
same applies when menopausal status is merely assumed from age: a 49-year-old may be
premenopausal or postmenopausal, and chemotherapy-induced amenorrhoea is not proof of
menopause because ovarian function can recover.

**Once status is established.** Premenopausal patients are generally offered tamoxifen for
five years, extended in higher-risk disease. Where risk warrants intensification, ovarian
function suppression may be added, and only in that combined setting does an aromatase
inhibitor become an option for a premenopausal patient. Postmenopausal patients are generally
offered an aromatase inhibitor, with tamoxifen reserved for those who cannot tolerate one.

**Comparative harms differ and matter to the conversation.** Tamoxifen carries
thromboembolic and endometrial risk; aromatase inhibitors carry arthralgia and accelerated
bone loss, so baseline bone density assessment is appropriate. Adherence over five to ten
years drives benefit more than the initial choice does, so tolerability is a legitimate
reason to switch classes.

Treatment decisions belong to the multidisciplinary team and the patient; decision-support
tools do not prescribe.
