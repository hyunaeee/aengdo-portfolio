## Diagnostic guidance (2025-09) · Nipple discharge — separating physiologic from pathologic

Scope: nipple discharge as the presenting complaint. The task is to sort discharge into
physiologic, endocrine, or pathologic before ordering anything. Evaluation of a
palpable mass and screening of asymptomatic women are covered elsewhere.

1. Characterize the discharge on six axes: spontaneous or only expressed; unilateral
   or bilateral; single duct orifice or multiple; color and consistency; presence of an
   associated mass or skin change; and the patient's age. These six answers determine
   the pathway.
2. Physiologic discharge is bilateral, arises from multiple duct orifices, appears only
   on expression, and is milky, yellow, green, or brown-grey. It is benign, frequently
   perpetuated by repeated self-expression, and needs no imaging beyond age-appropriate
   screening. Advise the patient to stop squeezing the nipple.
3. True galactorrhea — milky discharge in a woman who is neither pregnant nor recently
   lactating — is an endocrine problem, not a breast-imaging problem. Check serum
   prolactin, TSH, and a pregnancy test, and review medications that raise prolactin,
   including antipsychotics, metoclopramide, and opioids. Persistent hyperprolactinemia
   without a drug cause warrants pituitary MRI.
4. Pathologic discharge is spontaneous, unilateral, from a single duct, and clear,
   serous, serosanguineous, or frankly bloody. Any discharge meeting this description
   requires imaging regardless of how well the patient looks.
5. Image pathologic discharge with ultrasound focused on the retroareolar ducts, plus
   diagnostic mammography in patients aged 30 and over. Under 30, start with ultrasound
   and add mammography if ultrasound is abnormal.
6. If conventional imaging is negative but the discharge is convincingly pathologic,
   contrast-enhanced breast MRI is the next step; in most centers it has displaced
   galactography for localizing an occult intraductal lesion.
7. The commonest cause of pathologic discharge is a solitary intraductal papilloma,
   followed by duct ectasia. A minority represent DCIS or invasive carcinoma, and that
   minority rises with age.
8. Cytology of the discharge fluid has poor sensitivity. A negative smear never excludes
   malignancy and should not be used to close the episode.
9. Purulent discharge with periareolar inflammation, typically in a smoker, is
   periductal mastitis or abscess. Treat it as infection and reassess the duct system
   after the inflammation settles.
10. Persistent pathologic discharge with negative imaging is an indication for surgical
    duct excision — microdochectomy for a single identified duct, central duct excision
    in older patients with multi-duct disease. Counsel that excision may end the ability
    to breastfeed on that side.
