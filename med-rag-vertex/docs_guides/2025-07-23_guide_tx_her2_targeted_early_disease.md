## Treatment guidance (2025-07-23) · HER2-targeted therapy in early-stage disease

Scope: non-metastatic HER2-positive invasive breast cancer. This note covers antibody-based
HER2 therapy, its duration, and the monitoring it requires. It does not cover endocrine
therapy or radiotherapy planning.

**Confirm HER2 status before anything else.** Immunohistochemistry scored 3+ is positive;
0 or 1+ is negative; 2+ is equivocal and requires reflex in-situ hybridisation for
amplification. A 2+ result is not a licence to start treatment. Note also that "HER2-low"
(IHC 1+, or 2+ without amplification) is not HER2-positive and does not qualify a patient
for the regimens described here in the early setting.

**The backbone.** Standard treatment pairs chemotherapy with trastuzumab, given for a total
of roughly one year. Trastuzumab is not given as monotherapy in place of chemotherapy in
curative-intent treatment. For node-positive or larger tumours, dual antibody blockade adding
pertuzumab is commonly used, most often delivered before surgery so that response can be
observed. For small node-negative tumours — broadly T1a–T1b, under 1 cm — a de-escalated
regimen of weekly paclitaxel with trastuzumab is a reasonable option that avoids anthracycline
exposure.

**Post-neoadjuvant adaptation is the key decision point.** If a patient treated before surgery
achieves a pathological complete response, completing the planned year of trastuzumab-based
therapy is appropriate. If residual invasive disease is found in the breast or nodes, the
plan should change rather than continue unchanged: switching the remaining HER2 therapy to
the antibody-drug conjugate trastuzumab emtansine is the established response to residual
disease. Failing to act on a residual-disease result is a recognised care gap.

**Cardiac safety is not optional.** Obtain a baseline left ventricular ejection fraction by
echocardiogram or MUGA and repeat it approximately every three months during therapy.
Trastuzumab-related cardiac dysfunction is usually reversible if detected early, so a
meaningful fall in ejection fraction or new symptoms of heart failure prompts interruption and
cardiology input, not dose reduction alone. HER2 therapy is contraindicated in pregnancy;
effective contraception should be discussed before starting.

**If hormone receptors are also positive**, endocrine therapy is added after chemotherapy and
runs alongside the antibody, not instead of it.

Treatment decisions belong to the multidisciplinary team and the patient; decision-support
tools do not prescribe.
