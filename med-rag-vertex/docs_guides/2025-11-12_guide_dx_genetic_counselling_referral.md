## Diagnostic guidance (2025-11) · Referral for genetic counselling and germline testing

Scope: which breast clinic patients should be referred for hereditary cancer counselling
and germline testing, and what the result changes. This concerns inherited variants in
blood or saliva. It does not cover somatic tumor sequencing, and it does not cover
multigene prognostic assays used to decide adjuvant chemotherapy.

1. Counselling precedes testing and follows it. The pre-test conversation covers what a
   result would change, implications for relatives, and reproductive and insurance
   consequences; the post-test conversation translates the report into management.
2. Refer any patient diagnosed with breast cancer at or under 45 years, irrespective of
   family history.
3. Refer a diagnosis at or under 50 when there is also a close relative with breast,
   ovarian, pancreatic, or high-risk prostate cancer, or when the patient has had a
   second primary breast cancer.
4. Refer triple-negative breast cancer diagnosed up to about age 60 regardless of family
   history; the yield of a BRCA1 variant in this group is high enough to justify testing
   on histology alone.
5. Refer every male breast cancer, at any age.
6. Refer bilateral disease, particularly when the first primary occurred before
   menopause.
7. Refer on family structure: two or more close relatives on the same side of the family
   with breast cancer, any relative with ovarian carcinoma, a constellation of breast
   with pancreatic or metastatic prostate cancer, or Ashkenazi Jewish ancestry with any
   affected relative.
8. When a pathogenic variant is already known in the family, test the patient for that
   specific variant first. A broad panel is unnecessary and complicates interpretation.
9. Invasive lobular carcinoma with a family history of diffuse gastric cancer should
   prompt consideration of CDH1.
10. Panels typically report high-penetrance genes including BRCA1, BRCA2, and PALB2;
    moderate-penetrance genes such as ATM and CHEK2; and syndromic genes including TP53,
    PTEN, and STK11. Penetrance differs enormously between these tiers and management
    must follow the tier, not merely the presence of a name on the report.
11. A pathogenic result changes real decisions: it reframes the discussion of
    contralateral risk-reducing mastectomy, prompts risk-reducing salpingo-oophorectomy
    for BRCA carriers, supports annual MRI surveillance, may open PARP inhibitor
    eligibility, and enables cascade testing of relatives.
12. A variant of uncertain significance is not actionable. Never plan surgery on a VUS.
13. A negative panel in a patient with a strong family history does not return her to
    average risk; her empiric risk still comes from the pedigree.
