## Treatment guidance (2025-09-30) · Neoadjuvant therapy: patient selection and assessment of response

Scope: systemic therapy delivered before surgery in non-metastatic disease. This note covers
who should be offered it and how response is judged. It does not cover choice of individual
cytotoxic agents.

**Two separate reasons to treat before operating.** The first is surgical: a large tumour in a
patient who wants breast conservation, or bulky nodal disease, may become operable or
conservable after downstaging. The second, and increasingly the more important, is
informational — the response of the tumour to chemotherapy is a strong prognostic signal, and
in triple-negative and HER2-positive disease that signal directly changes what is given after
surgery. A patient with residual disease can be offered escalated post-operative treatment
that would never have been identified had they gone straight to theatre.

**Who is a candidate.** Triple-negative and HER2-positive tumours of roughly 2 cm or larger,
or with clinically involved nodes, are the clearest candidates. Inflammatory breast cancer is
always treated systemically first. By contrast, strongly hormone-receptor-positive,
HER2-negative, low-grade tumours respond poorly to chemotherapy and rarely achieve a complete
response, so pre-operative chemotherapy is usually not the right tool for them; upfront
surgery is generally preferred unless downstaging is specifically needed.

**Before the first cycle.** Establish histological diagnosis with a core needle biopsy —
never fine-needle aspiration alone — with full receptor and HER2 testing. Place a radiopaque
marker clip in the tumour bed and in any biopsy-proven abnormal node, because a complete
response can erase the target and leave the surgeon nothing to excise. Baseline imaging should
be documented, and staging investigations completed for stage III or clinically suspicious
presentations.

**Assessing response.** Clinical examination and imaging are used mid-treatment mainly to
detect progression, which mandates re-evaluation of the plan; they do not reliably predict
the final pathological result. MRI correlates better than mammography but still over- and
under-estimates residual disease. The definitive endpoint is pathology at surgery: a
pathological complete response means no residual invasive carcinoma in the breast and no
nodal involvement, with residual DCIS still counted as a complete response by the usual
convention. Residual disease should be quantified in a structured way and reported to the
tumour board, since it determines post-operative escalation.

Treatment decisions belong to the multidisciplinary team and the patient; decision-support
tools do not prescribe.
