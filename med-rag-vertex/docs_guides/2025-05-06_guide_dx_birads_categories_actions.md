## Diagnostic guidance (2025-05) · Reading BI-RADS assessment categories and the action each requires

Scope: how to interpret the final assessment category on a breast imaging report and
what management follows from it. This document concerns report interpretation only; it
does not tell you which imaging test to order first for a given symptom.

1. Category 0 — incomplete. The study cannot be finalized without additional views,
   ultrasound, or comparison with prior examinations. Category 0 is a workflow state,
   not a statement of risk. It is expected in screening recall and should be uncommon
   as the final result of a diagnostic study.
2. Category 1 — negative. Nothing to report. Return to the patient's routine screening
   interval.
3. Category 2 — benign. A specific benign entity is named: a coarsely calcified
   involuting fibroadenoma, a simple cyst, an intramammary lymph node, an oil cyst of
   fat necrosis, or a stable postsurgical scar. Management is identical to category 1 —
   routine screening — and no follow-up imaging is generated by the finding itself.
4. Category 3 — probably benign, carrying under roughly 2% likelihood of malignancy.
   The action is short-interval surveillance, conventionally at 6 months, then 12 and
   24 months, before the finding is downgraded to benign. Category 3 may be assigned
   only after a complete diagnostic workup, never off a screening study alone, and is
   inappropriate for a mass the patient can feel.
5. Category 4 — suspicious. Likelihood of malignancy spans roughly 2% to 95%, and
   tissue diagnosis is required. The subdivisions matter for counselling and for
   judging concordance afterwards: 4A low suspicion, 4B moderate suspicion, 4C high
   suspicion but short of classic malignant morphology.
6. Category 5 — highly suggestive of malignancy, at or above 95%. Biopsy is required,
   and surgical planning may proceed in parallel. A benign pathology result from a
   category 5 lesion is discordant by definition and mandates excision.
7. Category 6 — known biopsy-proven malignancy. It is applied to imaging performed
   after diagnosis, such as extent-of-disease mapping or response assessment during
   neoadjuvant therapy. It never means "biopsy again."
8. When a study shows several findings, the overall assessment takes the most abnormal
   category, and each breast is assessed separately.
9. Categories 4 and 5 require documented, tracked communication to the referring
   clinician; an unactioned suspicious report is a system failure, not a patient one.
