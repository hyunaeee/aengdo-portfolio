## Case record 2025-02-11 · 44F · palpable anterior neck nodule — THYROID, not breast

**Presentation.** A 44-year-old woman noticed a firm, painless lump in the lower
anterior neck while applying moisturizer. The mass moved on swallowing. No
hoarseness, no dysphagia, no compressive symptoms, no B symptoms. No family
history of thyroid or endocrine neoplasia; no prior neck irradiation. Bilateral
breast examination was normal and her most recent screening mammogram (BI-RADS 1)
was unremarkable — this case is **not** a breast presentation.

**Imaging.** Thyroid ultrasound showed a 1.7 cm solid hypoechoic nodule in the
right lobe with irregular margins, taller-than-wide orientation, and punctate
echogenic foci — ACR TI-RADS 5. Two subcentimetre spongiform nodules in the left
lobe were TI-RADS 2. Central compartment nodes were normal; one right level IV
lateral node showed rounded morphology and loss of the fatty hilum, raising
concern for nodal metastasis.

**Pathology.** Ultrasound-guided fine-needle aspiration of the dominant nodule
returned Bethesda category VI, positive for malignancy, favouring papillary
thyroid carcinoma. FNA of the suspicious level IV node was also positive, with a
washout thyroglobulin far above serum. TSH was normal at 1.4 mIU/L. Note that
ER/PR/HER2 receptor testing has **no role here**; thyroid carcinoma is
characterised by histologic subtype and molecular drivers such as BRAF V600E, not
by breast biomarker panels.

**Management.** Total thyroidectomy with therapeutic right lateral and central
neck dissection. Final staging pT1bN1b, classical papillary variant, no
extrathyroidal extension, margins negative. Preoperative and postoperative
laryngoscopy documented intact vocal cord mobility. Adjuvant radioactive iodine
was discussed at the endocrine tumour board; cytotoxic adjuvant chemotherapy was
explicitly **not** indicated. Levothyroxine was started with a TSH suppression
target, and surveillance planned with serum thyroglobulin, anti-thyroglobulin
antibodies, and neck ultrasound at six months to detect recurrence.

**Teaching point:** a palpable neck lump that elevates on swallowing is a thyroid
problem, not a breast problem — TI-RADS and Bethesda drive the workup, surgery is
thyroidectomy with node dissection, and any answer invoking sentinel lymph node
biopsy, endocrine therapy for hormone receptors, or breast adjuvant chemotherapy
protocols is retrieving the wrong disease.
