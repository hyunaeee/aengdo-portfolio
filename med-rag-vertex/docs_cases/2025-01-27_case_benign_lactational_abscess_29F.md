## Case record 2025-01-27 · 29F · lactational mastitis progressing to breast abscess

**Presentation.** A 29-year-old woman, five weeks postpartum and exclusively
breastfeeding, presented with two days of left breast pain, a hard tender area, fever to
38.6 °C, and flu-like aching. She reported difficulty with latch on the left and a
recently missed night feed. No trauma, no prior breast disease.

**Examination.** A wedge-shaped area of erythema, warmth, and induration over the left
upper outer quadrant, exquisitely tender, with a cracked nipple. Mildly tender reactive
left axillary node. No skin ulceration, no nipple retraction, no orange-peel change over
the whole breast.

**Initial management.** Diagnosed clinically as lactational mastitis. Treated with
effective milk removal (continued breastfeeding on the affected side plus expression),
analgesia, and oral cephalexin covering *Staphylococcus aureus*. Breastfeeding was
explicitly encouraged, not stopped.

**Escalation.** At 72 hours the fever persisted and a fluctuant area developed. Ultrasound
showed a 3.6 cm collection with thick irregular walls, internal debris and swirling echoes
on compression, and peripheral hyperaemia — a **lactational breast abscess**.
Ultrasound-guided needle aspiration drained 22 mL of purulent fluid; the catheter-free
aspiration was repeated once at 48 hours. Culture grew methicillin-sensitive *S. aureus*
sensitive to the prescribed agent. Surgical incision and drainage was avoided.

**Features pointing away from malignancy.** The context of lactation; acute onset over
days with systemic fever; a cracked nipple as a portal of entry; localised wedge-shaped
rather than whole-breast erythema; positive culture; and, most important, prompt and
complete clinical resolution once drained and treated.

**Red flags that would have changed the plan.** Inflammatory breast carcinoma is the
diagnosis that must not be missed. Warning features would have been: a non-lactating or
postmenopausal patient; erythema involving more than one third of the breast with peau
d'orange and no fever; failure of the inflammation to resolve after an adequate course of
antibiotics and drainage; a persistent underlying mass after resolution; matted or fixed
axillary nodes. Any of these mandates full-thickness skin punch biopsy plus core needle
biopsy of the breast parenchyma, not another antibiotic course.

**Course.** Afebrile by day 6; erythema resolved by day 12; breastfeeding continued
throughout. Follow-up ultrasound at 6 weeks showed no residual collection and no
underlying mass.

Teaching point: a lactating woman with fever and focal erythema has mastitis until an
abscess is found — but inflammation that does not clear after adequate treatment must be
biopsied to exclude inflammatory breast carcinoma.
