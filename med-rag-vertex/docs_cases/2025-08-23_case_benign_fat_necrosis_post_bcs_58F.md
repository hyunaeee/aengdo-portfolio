## Case record 2025-08-23 · 58F · fat necrosis at the lumpectomy bed, 16 months after BCS

**Presentation.** A 58-year-old woman, 16 months after breast-conserving surgery and
whole-breast radiotherapy for a 1.4 cm left-sided invasive ductal carcinoma (ER+ PR+
HER2−, pN0), on an aromatase inhibitor, presented with a newly firm, slightly irregular
area she could feel at the scar. It was mildly tender and had, in her words, "hardened"
over about two months. No new nipple discharge, no fever, no arm swelling.

**Examination.** A 2 cm firm plaque-like thickening directly beneath the lumpectomy scar
in the left upper inner quadrant, tethered to the scar but not to the chest wall, with
mild overlying skin retraction attributable to the surgical defect. No erythema. Axilla
clear.

**Imaging — why it looked frightening.** Diagnostic mammography showed a spiculated
density at the surgical bed, larger than on the 12-month study. This appearance overlaps
almost completely with local recurrence. Two findings argued otherwise: a central
**fat-containing radiolucency** within the density, and developing **coarse, curvilinear
rim calcification** around it — the evolving oil cyst pattern. Ultrasound was unhelpfully
suspicious: an irregular hypoechoic area with posterior acoustic shadowing. Contrast
MRI showed a lesion that was T1-hyperintense with signal loss on fat-saturated sequences,
with only thin peripheral rim enhancement and a persistent (type 1) kinetic curve, no
internal nodular enhancement.

**Tissue diagnosis.** Because imaging features overlapped with recurrence and the palpable
area was new, ultrasound-guided core needle biopsy was performed. Histology showed
anucleate fat cells, lipid-laden foamy histiocytes, multinucleated giant cells and
fibrosis, with no carcinoma. Radiologic–pathologic concordance was confirmed at the
multidisciplinary meeting. Diagnosis: **fat necrosis of the surgical bed.**

**Features pointing away from malignancy.** Location precisely at the operative bed; the
internal fat lucency on mammography; rim rather than pleomorphic calcification; lipid
signal on MRI; absent internal enhancement; and the absence of new axillary
lymphadenopathy.

**Red flags that would have changed the plan.** New fine pleomorphic or linear branching
microcalcifications; a solid enhancing nodule with washout (type 3) kinetics; progressive
interval growth on serial imaging; skin nodularity separate from the scar; or new
ipsilateral axillary nodes. Any of these would have been treated as in-breast recurrence
until biopsy proved otherwise, and would have triggered restaging rather than surveillance.

**Course.** Returned to routine annual mammographic surveillance; the lesion softened over
the following year.

Teaching point: fat necrosis after surgery or trauma is the classic benign mimic of
malignancy — a spiculated post-surgical density containing internal fat lucency with rim
calcification favours it, but a new palpable lump at the lumpectomy bed still earns a core
biopsy and a concordance check.
