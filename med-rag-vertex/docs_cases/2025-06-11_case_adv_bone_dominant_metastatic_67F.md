## Case record 2025-06-11 · 67F · bone-dominant metastatic lobular carcinoma, 9 years after breast-conserving surgery

**Presentation.** A 67-year-old woman reported three months of progressive low back pain, worse at
night and unrelieved by rest, without radicular symptoms, saddle anesthesia, or bladder
dysfunction. Nine years earlier she had breast-conserving surgery, sentinel node biopsy (0/2), and
whole-breast radiotherapy for a grade 1 invasive lobular carcinoma, pT1c pN0, ER 95%, PR 70%,
HER2 IHC 0, Ki-67 8%. She completed five years of letrozole and had been off endocrine therapy for
four years. Examination showed focal L3 tenderness with normal power, tone, and reflexes.

**Restaging workup.** Lumbar spine MRI showed marrow-replacing lesions at T12 and L3 with an
intact posterior vertebral wall, no epidural extension, and no cord compression. Whole-body bone
scintigraphy identified additional foci in the right iliac wing and two ribs. CT of chest,
abdomen, and pelvis found no hepatic, pulmonary, or nodal disease — the pattern was bone-dominant.
Alkaline phosphatase was elevated at 214 U/L; corrected calcium, renal function, and hemoglobin
were normal.

**Biopsy confirmation.** CT-guided core biopsy of the L3 lesion returned metastatic lobular
carcinoma with characteristic loss of E-cadherin, ER 95%, PR 40%, HER2 IHC 0 — concordant with the
primary. Circulating tumor DNA showed no ESR1 mutation and wild-type PIK3CA.

**Management decision.** This is incurable but indolent, endocrine-sensitive disease, and the goal
is durable disease control with preserved skeletal integrity rather than cure. Because relapse
occurred well over a year after completing an aromatase inhibitor, she was still considered
endocrine-sensitive; first-line therapy was a CDK4/6 inhibitor combined with an aromatase
inhibitor, not chemotherapy. A bone-modifying agent (denosumab every four weeks) was started with
calcium and vitamin D and a dental clearance for osteonecrosis risk. A single 8 Gy palliative
fraction was delivered to L3 for pain, and spinal surgery review confirmed a stable lesion not
requiring instrumentation.

**Teaching point.** At first presentation the primary tumor defines biology and surgery is
central; here surgery has no role, the metastatic biopsy governs treatment, and the disease is
managed as a chronic illness. Bone-dominant, hormone-receptor-positive relapse with a long
disease-free interval should not be escalated to cytotoxic therapy simply because it is stage IV.
