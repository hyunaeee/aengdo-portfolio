## Case record 2025-06-19 · 27F · tender axillary lymphadenopathy — INFECTIOUS, not malignant

**Presentation.** A 27-year-old woman presented with a tender lump in the right
axilla for ten days. She reported low-grade fever to 37.9 °C and malaise. She
adopted a kitten six weeks earlier and recalled a scratch on the right forearm
that had left a small crusted papule. No breast lump, no nipple discharge, no
skin dimpling; bilateral breast examination was entirely normal, and this is
**not** a breast cancer case despite the axillary vocabulary.

**Examination and imaging.** Two mobile, tender right axillary nodes, the largest
2.4 cm, with mild overlying erythema and no fluctuance. Epitrochlear fullness on
the same arm. Axillary ultrasound showed enlarged nodes with preserved fatty
hilum, uniform cortical thickness of 3 mm, and hilar vascularity on Doppler —
a reactive pattern. Ipsilateral whole-breast ultrasound was normal. Because of her
age, mammography was not indicated; targeted ultrasound was the appropriate
imaging modality.

**Workup.** White cell count was 9.8 × 10⁹/L with mild lymphocytosis; CRP 34 mg/L.
*Bartonella henselae* IgG titre was elevated at 1:512. HIV, EBV, and tuberculosis
testing were negative, and there was no travel or livestock exposure. Because
imaging was reassuringly reactive and a plausible infectious source existed, core
biopsy was deferred rather than performed up front; the plan specified biopsy only
if the nodes failed to regress. No hormone receptor or HER2 testing was ordered —
there is no carcinoma in this record.

**Course.** Cat scratch disease was diagnosed and a five-day course of oral
azithromycin given with analgesia. At four weeks the largest node had shrunk to
1.1 cm and tenderness resolved; at twelve weeks the axilla was normal by palpation
and ultrasound. No surgery, no sentinel lymph node biopsy, no axillary dissection,
and no adjuvant chemotherapy were required at any point. She was counselled that
persistent, hard, or progressively enlarging nodes would mandate tissue diagnosis.

**Teaching point:** tender axillary lymphadenopathy in a young patient with
preserved fatty hilum on ultrasound and an infectious exposure is reactive and is
managed with antibiotics and interval follow-up — clinically the opposite of the
firm, hilum-effaced node of nodal metastasis, and never an indication for axillary
surgery or oncologic staging.
