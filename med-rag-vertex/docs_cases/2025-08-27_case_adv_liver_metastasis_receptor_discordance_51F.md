## Case record 2025-08-27 · 51F · liver-dominant metastasis with receptor discordance from the primary

**Presentation.** A 51-year-old woman described six weeks of right upper quadrant discomfort,
early satiety, 6 kg unintentional weight loss, and fatigue. Four years earlier she was treated for
right-sided grade 3 IDC, pT2 pN1a, ER 80%, PR 15%, HER2 IHC 1+ (not amplified), Ki-67 40%, with
breast-conserving surgery, adjuvant anthracycline-taxane chemotherapy, whole-breast radiotherapy,
and ongoing letrozole with ovarian function suppression. Examination found a firm liver edge 4 cm
below the costal margin without jaundice or ascites.

**Restaging workup.** Alkaline phosphatase was 340 U/L with mildly elevated transaminases and
normal bilirubin. Contrast CT showed at least seven hypodense hepatic lesions, the largest 4.2 cm
in segment VII, plus a solitary 8 mm right lower lobe lung nodule. FDG PET/CT confirmed
liver-dominant disease with no skeletal or CNS-suspicious findings. Brain imaging was deferred as
she was neurologically asymptomatic.

**Biopsy confirmation.** Ultrasound-guided core biopsy of the segment VII lesion returned
metastatic carcinoma of breast origin (GATA3 positive, CK7 positive), but with a different
receptor profile: ER 0%, PR 0%, HER2 IHC 3+. The archived primary block was re-cut and re-tested
in the same laboratory and remained ER 80%, HER2 IHC 1+ with a non-amplified FISH ratio,
confirming true biologic discordance — loss of hormone receptor expression with gain of HER2
overexpression — rather than an assay artifact.

**Management decision.** Endocrine therapy was stopped, because it can no longer act on an
ER-negative clone. She was reclassified as HER2-positive metastatic disease and started on a
taxane with trastuzumab and pertuzumab, after baseline echocardiography (LVEF 62%) and hepatitis B
serology. Germline BRCA1/2 testing was sent for future treatment sequencing. The lung nodule was
kept under CT surveillance rather than biopsied, since it would not alter the systemic plan.

**Teaching point.** Unlike a first presentation, where the resected primary defines treatment for
years, the biology of relapsed disease must be re-established from the metastasis itself. Had the
liver lesions been treated on the archived ER-positive, HER2-negative profile, this patient would
have received endocrine therapy her disease could not respond to and been denied HER2-directed
treatment. Re-biopsy at first distant relapse is the decision point.
