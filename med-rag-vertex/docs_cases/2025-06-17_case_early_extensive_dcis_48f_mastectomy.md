## Case record 2025-06-17 · 48F · extensive segmental calcifications, high-grade DCIS with microinvasion

**Presentation.** A 48-year-old perimenopausal woman with irregular cycles was recalled from
screening. She had no palpable abnormality, no nipple discharge, no pain, and no family
history of breast cancer. Her previous screening study three years earlier had been reported
as normal.

**Workup.** Magnification views of the right breast showed fine linear and fine-branching
pleomorphic calcifications in a segmental distribution spanning approximately 6 cm from the
retroareolar region toward the upper outer quadrant, assessed BI-RADS 4C. Ultrasound showed
no discrete mass and a normal axilla. Stereotactic vacuum-assisted biopsy sampled two
separate calcification clusters 4 cm apart, each marked with a clip. Breast MRI demonstrated
non-mass enhancement in a segmental pattern matching the calcification extent, with no
separate enhancing mass and no contralateral abnormality.

**Pathology.** Both biopsy sites showed high-grade ductal carcinoma in situ with comedo-type
necrosis. No invasive component was identified on core material. ER was 90% positive on the
in situ component.

**Course.** A 6 cm segmental extent in a modest-volume breast made a cosmetically acceptable
excision with clear margins unlikely, and the disease crossed quadrant boundaries, so total
(simple) mastectomy was recommended rather than attempted conservation. Sentinel lymph node
biopsy was performed at the same operation — not because in situ disease itself spreads to
nodes, but because mastectomy removes the option of lymphatic mapping later should occult
invasion be found. Final pathology showed 5.5 cm of high-grade DCIS with a single 1.2 mm
focus of microinvasion, clear margins, and 0/2 sentinel nodes negative — pT1mi pN0(sn).
Post-mastectomy radiotherapy was not indicated with clear margins and negative nodes, and
chemotherapy was not indicated for microinvasive node-negative disease. She was offered
tamoxifen for five years to reduce contralateral and chest-wall risk, and started it after
discussion of endometrial and thromboembolic risks. Delayed autologous reconstruction was
referred, and she remains under annual contralateral mammography.

**Teaching point.** DCIS management is driven by extent and by whether clear margins are
achievable, not by grade alone — widely segmental calcifications push the decision toward
mastectomy, and sentinel node biopsy is taken at that operation because the mapping
opportunity cannot be recovered afterwards.
