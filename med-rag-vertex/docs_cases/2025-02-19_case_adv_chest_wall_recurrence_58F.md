## Case record 2025-02-19 · 58F · isolated chest-wall recurrence 6.5 years after mastectomy

**Presentation.** A 58-year-old postmenopausal woman was referred from surveillance clinic with
two firm, non-tender pink nodules in the right mastectomy scar, measuring 6 mm and 9 mm, noticed
over roughly eight weeks. Her primary disease, treated 6.5 years earlier, was a grade 2 invasive
ductal carcinoma (IDC), pT2 pN1a (2 of 14 nodes), ER 90%, PR 60%, HER2 IHC 0, Ki-67 12%. She
underwent right total mastectomy with axillary lymph node dissection, adjuvant
docetaxel/cyclophosphamide, and five years of anastrozole. Post-mastectomy radiotherapy was
discussed at the original tumor board and not delivered. She has been off all systemic therapy
for 18 months. No bone pain, no dyspnea, no weight loss.

**Restaging workup.** Chest-wall ultrasound showed two hypoechoic dermal-subdermal nodules
without deep pectoral invasion. Contralateral mammography was BI-RADS 1. Staging FDG PET/CT
demonstrated uptake confined to the two cutaneous nodules, with no internal mammary,
supraclavicular, visceral, or skeletal uptake. Liver function tests, calcium, and alkaline
phosphatase were normal.

**Biopsy confirmation.** Punch biopsy of the larger nodule returned invasive carcinoma
morphologically matching the archived primary: ER 85%, PR 30%, HER2 IHC 0. Receptor profile was
therefore concordant with the primary, and no change of biologic class was implied.

**Management decision.** The multidisciplinary board treated this as an isolated locoregional
recurrence with curative intent, not as metastatic disease. Plan: wide local excision of both
nodules to negative margins, followed by chest-wall and supraclavicular radiotherapy — feasible
because the chest wall had never been irradiated. Because the recurrence is strongly
hormone-receptor positive, systemic management was a switch of endocrine class rather than
cytotoxic therapy; she began fulvestrant with a CDK4/6 inhibitor after cardiology and bone-density
baselines. Chemotherapy was explicitly not recommended, as its benefit in resected locoregional
recurrence is concentrated in hormone-receptor-negative disease.

**Teaching point.** Unlike a first presentation, where staging establishes biology from an
untreated primary, this decision turned on what she had already received: the absence of prior
chest-wall radiotherapy made curative-intent local therapy possible, and prior aromatase
inhibitor exposure dictated the endocrine switch. An isolated chest-wall recurrence is a
potentially curable event and must not be managed as stage IV disease.
