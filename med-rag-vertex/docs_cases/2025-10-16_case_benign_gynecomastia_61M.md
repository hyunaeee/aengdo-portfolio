## Case record 2025-10-16 · 61M · drug-induced gynecomastia versus male breast cancer

**Presentation.** A 61-year-old man with heart failure with reduced ejection fraction
reported four months of tender swelling behind both nipples, worse on the left. He had
been started on spironolactone seven months earlier. No nipple discharge, no weight loss,
no testicular symptoms. No family history of breast or ovarian cancer and no known
pathogenic *BRCA2* variant. Alcohol intake modest; no cannabis, no anabolic steroids.

**Examination.** Bilateral, mildly asymmetric, rubbery, tender subareolar discs of tissue —
about 4 cm on the left and 2 cm on the right — **concentric to and centred on the nipple**,
mobile over the pectoral fascia. No skin dimpling, no nipple retraction or ulceration, no
discharge on expression. Both axillae normal. Testicular examination normal, no signs of
chronic liver disease.

**Imaging.** Bilateral mammography showed a flame-shaped/nodular subareolar density
fanning from the nipple into the surrounding fat, symmetric in pattern, without a discrete
mass, spiculation, or microcalcification. Ultrasound showed hypoechoic subareolar
fibroglandular tissue radiating from the nipple with no discrete solid lesion.
Assessment: **BI-RADS 2, gynecomastia (여성형 유방).** No biopsy performed.

**Laboratory.** Testosterone, LH, hCG, oestradiol, TSH, and liver and renal function were
checked to exclude a secondary endocrine cause; all were within reference range, supporting
a drug effect.

**Features pointing away from malignancy.** Bilaterality; tenderness (male breast cancer
is characteristically painless); tissue centred symmetrically under the nipple rather than
eccentric to it; soft-to-rubbery mobile consistency; a clear temporal relationship to a
known causative drug; and absence of nipple or skin change and of axillary nodes.

**Red flags that would have changed the plan.** An **eccentric**, hard, fixed mass not
centred beneath the nipple; unilateral painless enlargement; nipple retraction, ulceration
or bloody discharge; skin fixation; or palpable axillary lymphadenopathy. Any of these
would have required diagnostic mammography plus ultrasound and **core needle biopsy**,
since roughly 1% of breast cancers occur in men, are usually ER-positive invasive ductal
carcinoma, and present at a more advanced stage because of delayed recognition.

**Course.** Spironolactone was switched to eplerenone after discussion with cardiology.
Tenderness settled within eight weeks and the subareolar tissue had regressed noticeably by
the four-month review.

Teaching point: gynecomastia is tender, bilateral and concentric under the nipple, and
usually drug-related — an eccentric, hard, painless unilateral mass in a man is male breast
cancer until core biopsy says otherwise.
