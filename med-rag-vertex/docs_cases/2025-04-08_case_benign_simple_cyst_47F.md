## Case record 2025-04-08 · 47F · tender palpable lump, aspirated simple cyst

**Presentation.** A 47-year-old premenopausal woman, para 2, presented with a lump in the
left breast at the 3 o'clock position that had appeared over about ten days and felt
tender and "full." She had noticed similar lumps twice before, both of which had resolved
spontaneously. No nipple discharge, no skin change, no fever, no trauma. Last screening
mammogram 14 months earlier reported as BI-RADS 1.

**Examination.** A smooth, round, mildly tender, well-defined mass approximately 3 cm,
slightly compressible, mobile. No erythema or warmth (excluding infection). No nipple
inversion. Axilla clear.

**Imaging.** Diagnostic ultrasound showed an anechoic lesion, 31 × 28 mm, with an
imperceptible thin wall, sharply circumscribed margins, posterior acoustic enhancement,
and no internal echoes, septations, or mural nodule. No vascularity within the lesion.
Assessment: **BI-RADS 2, simple cyst.** Diagnostic mammography of the same quadrant showed
only a circumscribed equal-density oval — concordant.

**Features pointing away from malignancy.** Anechoic contents with through-transmission;
imperceptible wall; complete absence of a solid component; compressibility; rapid onset
with fluctuation over previous cycles; and, decisively, complete resolution after
aspiration.

**Procedure and course.** Because the cyst was symptomatic, ultrasound-guided fine-needle
aspiration was performed and yielded 14 mL of clear straw-coloured fluid. The mass
disappeared completely on immediate post-aspiration scanning. The fluid was **not** sent
for cytology: non-bloody aspirate with total resolution of a sonographically simple cyst
does not require cytological examination. She was reviewed at 8 weeks with no
reaccumulation.

**Red flags that would have changed the plan.** Blood-stained aspirate; failure of the
mass to resolve completely after aspiration; a residual solid component, thick septation,
or mural nodule on ultrasound (i.e. a complex cystic and solid mass rather than a simple
cyst); rapid reaccumulation over days; or associated skin thickening. Any of these would
have prompted core needle biopsy of the solid element rather than reassurance, since
intracystic papillary carcinoma and cystic-necrotic invasive carcinoma present this way.

Teaching point: a strictly anechoic, thin-walled, circumscribed lesion with posterior
enhancement is a simple cyst (BI-RADS 2) and needs no tissue diagnosis — but a bloody
aspirate or a residual mass after drainage converts the encounter into a biopsy pathway.
