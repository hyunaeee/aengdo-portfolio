## Case record 2025-06-19 · 44F · cyclical mastalgia with focal nodularity, imaging performed

**Presentation.** A 44-year-old perimenopausal woman described 14 months of bilateral
breast heaviness and soreness beginning 7–10 days before menses and easing within two days
of onset of bleeding. Cycles had become irregular over the past year. She was not on
hormone therapy or combined oral contraception. The distinguishing feature from ordinary
cyclical pain was a persistent area of lumpiness in the left upper outer quadrant that she
could feel throughout the cycle.

**Examination.** Diffuse bilateral tenderness. In the left upper outer quadrant there was
an ill-defined region of granular nodularity roughly 4 cm across without a discrete
dominant mass, with no skin tethering and no nipple change. Axillae clear.

**Why imaging was performed here.** Unlike a woman in her twenties with purely bilateral
cyclical pain and a normal examination, this patient had two features that lowered the
threshold for imaging: age over 40 and a palpable focal abnormality that persisted across
the cycle. Bilateral digital mammography showed heterogeneously dense parenchyma
(category C) with no mass, distortion, or suspicious calcification. Targeted ultrasound of
the palpable area showed clustered microcysts and mild ductal ectasia consistent with
fibrocystic change, no solid nodule, no shadowing. Combined assessment: **BI-RADS 2.**

**Features pointing away from malignancy.** Bilaterality; strict temporal coupling to the
luteal phase with resolution after menstruation; nodularity that was granular and diffuse
rather than a discrete dominant mass; no skin or nipple change; and concordant negative
mammography plus ultrasound in a symptomatic quadrant.

**Red flags that would have changed the plan.** Pain that was unilateral, fixed to one
point, non-cyclical and progressive; a discrete dominant mass within the nodular area;
spontaneous unilateral serosanguineous nipple discharge; skin thickening or peau d'orange;
new nipple inversion; or a suspicious sonographic correlate. Any of these would have
required core needle biopsy of the focal lesion, regardless of the pain pattern.

**Course.** Managed with a properly fitted supportive bra, topical diclofenac to the
tender areas during the luteal phase, and a symptom diary over three cycles. Pain score
fell from 7/10 to 3/10. No endocrine agent (danazol or tamoxifen) was needed. Reviewed at
6 months, unchanged.

Teaching point: cyclical mastalgia is benign, but focal persistent nodularity in a woman
over 40 earns imaging — the pain is reassuring, the lump is what must be cleared.
