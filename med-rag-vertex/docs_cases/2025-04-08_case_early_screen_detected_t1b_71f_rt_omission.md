## Case record 2025-04-08 · 71F · screen-detected 9 mm luminal A carcinoma, radiotherapy omission

**Presentation.** A 71-year-old postmenopausal woman attended routine screening mammography
with no breast symptoms. She had never taken menopausal hormone therapy, had two children
with first birth at 26, and reported no family history of breast or ovarian cancer.
Comorbidities were well-controlled hypertension and osteopenia under surveillance.

**Workup.** Screening mammography showed a new 9 mm irregular mass with indistinct margins
in the lower inner quadrant of the left breast, absent on the prior study two years earlier;
background density was scattered fibroglandular. Diagnostic magnification and spot-compression
views confirmed a persistent mass with no associated calcifications. Targeted ultrasound
demonstrated a 9 mm hypoechoic mass, taller-than-wide but without posterior shadowing,
assessed BI-RADS 4A. Axillary ultrasound was normal, with all visualised nodes showing
preserved fatty hila. Ultrasound-guided core biopsy with clip placement was performed. No MRI
was obtained; the lesion was well characterised and the breast was not dense.

**Pathology.** Invasive ductal carcinoma, grade 1, with prominent tubule formation. ER 95%,
PR 80%, HER2 IHC 1+ (negative), Ki-67 8%. No lymphovascular invasion.

**Course.** Wire-localised breast-conserving surgery with sentinel lymph node biopsy. Final
pathology: 8 mm invasive carcinoma, all margins clear, 0/2 sentinel nodes negative — pT1b
pN0. A 21-gene recurrence score assay returned a low result, so adjuvant chemotherapy was not
offered. The multidisciplinary discussion then centred on radiotherapy. With age above 70, a
tumour under 2 cm, node-negative and strongly ER-positive disease, clear margins, and a
patient willing to commit to endocrine therapy, whole-breast irradiation was presented as
optional: it reduces in-breast recurrence but has not demonstrated a survival advantage in
this subgroup. After counselling she chose to omit radiotherapy and began anastrozole for
five years, with baseline DEXA and calcium/vitamin D supplementation given her osteopenia.
Annual mammography and six-monthly clinical review were arranged, with adherence stressed —
omitting radiotherapy is only defensible if the endocrine therapy is actually taken.

**Teaching point.** A small, low-grade, strongly ER-positive, node-negative cancer in a woman
over 70 is the specific setting in which radiotherapy may reasonably be omitted after
breast-conserving surgery — accepting a higher local recurrence rate in exchange for reduced
treatment burden, and contingent on endocrine adherence.
